The Interview Mindset: Reframing the Pressure

Most candidates approach interviews from entirely the wrong psychological position. They walk in thinking: "I hope they like me. I hope I say the right things. I hope I don't mess this up." This mindset places all the power with the interviewer and sets you up for a performance driven by anxiety rather than confidence. The most successful candidates reframe the interview as a mutual assessment. You are evaluating whether this company and role is right for you, just as they are evaluating whether you are right for them.

This reframe does something remarkable: it transforms your emotional state. Instead of desperate-to-please anxiety, you access genuine curiosity. You ask better questions. You listen more carefully. You present as confident and discerning rather than eager and nervous. Paradoxically, this makes you far more attractive as a candidate. Research from behavioural economists at Harvard shows that candidates who demonstrate selectivity, who signal they have other options and high standards, consistently receive better offers than equally qualified candidates who appear desperate. The practical implication: walk into every interview having done thorough research, having genuine questions prepared, and having made a conscious decision that this role is worth your serious consideration. This is not performance, it's the honest truth, and it comes through authentically.

02

The STAR Framework: Answering Behavioural Questions

Behavioural questions, "Tell me about a time when...", "Give me an example of...", account for the majority of questions in competency-based interviews, which are now the standard format for graduate roles at most major employers. These questions are designed to predict future behaviour based on past behaviour, and they have a specific structure that rewards candidates who answer with concrete, specific, well-organised stories.

The STAR framework, Situation, Task, Action, Result, is the most effective structure for these answers. Situation: set the scene in two to three sentences. What was the context? What was at stake? Task: clarify your specific responsibility. What were you personally required to do? Action: this is the most important element and should take up at least half your answer time. Describe specifically what you did, not what the team did, not what happened, but your individual actions, decisions, and reasoning. Result: quantify the outcome where possible. What changed? What was achieved? What did you learn? Prepare six to eight STAR stories before any interview, covering the core competencies most employers assess: leadership, problem-solving, communication, teamwork, resilience, initiative, and decision-making under pressure. Each story should be flexible enough to answer multiple questions.

03

Technical Interview Mastery

Technical interviews test not just your knowledge but your ability to think clearly under pressure, communicate your reasoning, and collaborate with the person interviewing you. The biggest mistake candidates make in technical interviews is staying silent while they think. Interviewers are not just evaluating your answer, they are evaluating how you think. Narrate your thought process continuously: "My first instinct is X, but let me think about why that might not work..." This shows intellectual honesty and collaborative thinking, which are the qualities technical interviewers most value.

For coding interviews, always clarify the problem before writing a single line: ask about edge cases, confirm input and output formats, and state your initial approach before implementing it. This demonstrates the kind of careful, deliberate thinking that senior engineers look for in collaborators. Start with the brute-force solution even if you know a more efficient one exists, then optimise. This shows you understand trade-offs rather than just memorising answers. For system design interviews, always structure your approach: requirements gathering, high-level design, component detail, trade-offs. For case study interviews used in consulting and finance roles, practice the MECE principle, Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive, which structures your analysis so it covers all relevant factors without overlap or gaps. The most important preparation tool is mock interviews with a real human, not just practising in your head.

04

200 Questions You Must Be Ready For

The universe of interview questions is smaller than most candidates realise. Despite the infinite apparent variety, most questions fall into six categories. First: self-assessment questions, "Tell me about yourself", "What are your strengths and weaknesses?", "Why are you interested in this role?" These require a confident, prepared narrative about your identity, trajectory, and motivation. Second: behavioural questions, covered by your STAR stories. Third: situational questions, "What would you do if..." These test your judgment and values, not experience. Answer by demonstrating your decision-making framework.

Fourth: company and role questions, "What do you know about us?", "Where do you see this industry in five years?" These test preparation and genuine interest. Research the company's recent news, financial results, strategic priorities, and competitors before every interview. Fifth: technical questions, specific to your field, testing knowledge and reasoning. Sixth: questions for the interviewer. This is where most candidates miss a huge opportunity. Your questions should demonstrate strategic thinking: "What does success look like in this role after six months?" or "What are the biggest challenges facing the team right now?" or "What do the people who thrive here have in common?" Never ask questions answered on the website, and never ask about salary or benefits in a first interview unless the interviewer raises it. Prepare at least five questions and use at least three.

05

The Day Before and Day Of: Performance Preparation

Elite athletes don't wing their performance on the day, they have meticulous pre-competition routines that put them in peak physical and mental condition. You should approach interviews the same way. The day before: confirm all logistics (location, time, interviewer names), review your research notes, re-read the job description, do one final STAR story rehearsal out loud, prepare your clothes, and get a full night's sleep. Sleep is not optional, cognitive function and verbal fluency are measurably degraded by sleep deprivation, and interviewers notice.

The morning of: eat a proper meal, avoid excessive caffeine, and give yourself substantial travel buffer, arriving flustered because of a delayed train is entirely avoidable. Arrive at least ten minutes early but not more than fifteen, wait outside or in a nearby café if necessary. While waiting, review your key points mentally. In the first thirty seconds after being greeted, make a strong first impression: firm handshake, genuine smile, eye contact, and a brief warm opener. Research by Princeton psychologists shows that judgments formed in the first thirty seconds are remarkably persistent and difficult to override. After the interview: send a personalised thank-you email within 24 hours, referencing a specific topic from the conversation. This is done by fewer than 10% of candidates and is noticed by almost all hiring managers.

06

Salary Negotiation and Offer Handling

The interview process doesn't end when you receive an offer, it ends when you sign a contract you're genuinely satisfied with. Salary negotiation is one of the highest-return skills you can develop. A single successful negotiation in your first job adds compounding value to every subsequent salary discussion throughout your career, because percentage-based increases are calculated from your base. Yet fewer than 40% of candidates negotiate their first offer, most because they don't know how or fear damaging the relationship.

The data is clear: employers almost never rescind offers because a candidate negotiated respectfully. In fact, negotiating often increases an employer's perception of your confidence and self-awareness. The framework is simple. Receive the offer warmly and enthusiastically, "This is really exciting, thank you", then ask for time: "Could I have a couple of days to review the full package?" Use that time to research market rates using Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, and industry salary surveys. Return with a specific counter: "Based on my research and the skills I bring, I was hoping we could explore £[X]. Is there flexibility there?" Always give a reason, it makes the request more human and easier to accommodate. If they can't move on base salary, ask about start date, title, remote working flexibility, training budget, or performance review timing. Something is almost always negotiable, even in structured graduate schemes.

⚡ Key Takeaways
  • Reframe interviews as mutual assessment, confidence comes from genuine selectivity.
  • Master six to eight STAR stories flexible enough to answer any behavioural question.
  • In technical interviews, narrate your thinking continuously, process matters as much as result.
  • Prepare five strategic questions for the interviewer, it signals serious interest and intelligence.
  • Always negotiate your offer, respectful negotiation almost never damages relationships.
